Academic Papers

  • Trends in Household Debt and Credit Handbook of US Consumer Economics, Chapter 2 · Andrew Haughwout, Donghoon Lee, Joelle Scally, Lauren Thomas, Wilbert van der Klaauw · Federal Reserve Bank of New York · August 2019

    We examine trends in household debt before, during, and since the 2000s financial crisis and Great Recession — a period unique in American history. Prior to the Great Recession, a historic run-up in housing debt coincided with a speculative bubble; as prices fell, millions of households defaulted on their mortgages, greatly contributing to the deepest recession since the 1930s. Following the steep increase in debt during the boom, households rapidly paid off loans in the years that followed. Since 2013, debt has begun rising again, though at a slower rate, at least partially due to stricter lending standards. We find that while total household debt has recovered to its previous level in nominal terms, its composition and characteristics have changed dramatically along many dimensions.

Academic Theses

  • Developing an Early-Warning Gentrification System with Machine Learning University of Oxford · MSc in Social Data Science · 2021 · [PDF]

    This study builds an 'early-warning gentrification system' to help policymakers act before displacement occurs — a limitation of most traditional measures, which are collected too slowly to be useful in time. Using data from Twitter, 311 complaints, crime rates, address vacancies, and socioeconomic features commonly used in past research, I predict which census tracts would gentrify among 405 eligible tracts in NYC between 2010–2018. I extract structured and unstructured features from Twitter using a Mallet LDA topic model, then build random forest and logistic regression models tuned with tenfold cross-validation. The full model substantially outperforms baselines, with gentrification linked to topics such as employment, politics, sports, and the use of slang.

  • The Dual Role of Political Identity and Nationality in Cooperation Université Lumière Lyon 2 · MSc in Economics · 2020 · [PDF]

    Although several studies have investigated the role of social identity in economics, few have examined how multiple facets of identity reinforce, or alternatively, undermine, in-group biases and out-group discrimination. This study contributes to the sparse literature on this question by investigating whether sharing a common American nationality can overcome partisan discrimination against members of the political out-group. I randomize 57 American participants into one of two treatment arms: a priming treatment that attempts to increase the salience of their national identity by asking them to reflect upon their country and its culture, and a baseline treatment inquiring about their favorite TV shows that serves as a control. After the treatment, all participants play a standard stag hunt game against six hypothetical opponents: two American, two Brits, and two Frenchmen/women, each of whom is identified as either a Republican or Democrat. The participants are then paired off and awarded points based on their actual choices in the study. I find that my participants frequently demonstrate a strong preference for their co-partisans, regardless of nationality; the priming treatment does not appear to affect this pattern. I also observe that partisan discrimination seems to be driven mostly by statistical discrimination rather than a distaste for members of the opposite party, although the latter plays a small role as well.

  • For Love or Money: Why Students Choose Careers in Finance and Consulting Northwestern University · BA in Economics & Political Science · 2017 · [PDF]

    For elite university students, finance and consulting are the two most popular post-graduation destinations, despite the best effort of universities to steer their graduates into other, more "socially useful" paths. I investigate this phenomenon by administering a survey to 97 Northwestern University students that measures their subjective expectations about job outcomes, conditional on job industry, as well as other demographic and personal characteristics that have been identified in previous research as potential causes for preferring jobs in finance or consulting. I examine two outcome variables: applying to a job in finance or consulting, and receiving a job offer in those industries. I derive a novel discrete choice random utility model where decisions about the job application process are made with limited and uncertain information. I also estimate a binomial logit model to examine the impact of individuals' personal and demographic characteristics on job offers. I find that students have heterogenous and highly uncertain beliefs about future job outcomes, although uncertainty is reduced as students receive more information. Students' expectations also become more accurate as they receive more information, consistent with prior research. Finally, I find that demographics (family income, race, gender, major), as well as the promise of future job offers, are significantly correlated with the application and job offer decisions.
